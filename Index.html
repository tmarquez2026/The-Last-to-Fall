Henry Gunther's Biography
        World War 1Â War ended on the 11th hour of the 11th day of the 11th month of 1918. A corpse dropped in a French village as the bells tolled and the guns fell quiet. There was Henry Nicholas John Gunther, an American soldier who was likely the last soldier killed by any of the belligerents during World War I. He was killed at 10:59 a.m., only one minute before the Armistice was to take effect at 11:00 a.m.

        Henry Gunther was born on June 6, 1895, in east Baltimore, Maryland, to a German-American family. George Gunther (1869-1919) and Lina Roth (1866-1938) were both German immigrant children. He was born in Highlandtown, an East Baltimore area primarily impacted by German immigrants, where his family belonged to the Roman Catholic parish of Sacred Heart of Jesus. In the National Bank of Baltimore, Henry Gunther worked as a bookkeeper and clerk. In 1915, he joined the Knights of Columbus, a Roman Catholic lay service order.

Military Service

        Even though Gunther was not immediately enlisted because of his heritage, he was enlisted then promoted as supply sergeant. But due to him telling his friend not to get drafted he was demoted to private. The morning of November 11 was still filled with fighting even though the Armistice with Germany was signed by 5 am but it would not come to force until 11 am. His squad reached a roadblock with 2 german machine guns. Gunther got up and the german soldiers who were aware of the Armistice tried to wave Gunther away. He kept going and fired a shot or two and when he got too close to the machine guns, he was shot and killed instantly. The Army restored his rank of Sergeant and awarded him Divisional Citation for Gallantry in Action and Distinguished Service Cross.

Memorials


        On November 11, 2008, "Veterans Day," also known as "Armistice Day" in France, a memorial was built near the site where Gunther died in Chaumont-devant-Damvillers, Lorraine. On the same remembrance holiday observance two years later, November 11, 2010, a memorial plaque was likewise unveiled at his grave site in America at 10:59 a.m by the German Society of Maryland.
